Word2Vec Model

Word2Vec is a model that takes sequences of words representing documents and turns them into features available for training and testing. The basis for the model relies on the idea that similar words in meaning will be next to each other, and dissimilar words will be far apart.

Generating Training Set

In order to extract features from a sequence of words, Word2Vec first generates a training set of word pairings from all documents. The user defines the size of a “window” to look for word pairs, where words inside the window would be considered to be similar. To provide an example, let’s take a look at the sentence: “The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog.” The word pairing scheme is illustrated in the below image:

Here we see a window of size 2, where words that are of distance 2 are considered to make word pairs. The word pairs serve as input and ouput sets for training.

Using Neural Network (Sort of)

Now that we have a training set, it’s time to “train” a model. The model we will use is a neural network, but we are not actually interested in training a neural network. Instead, we take advantage of the neural network’s structure, which consists of an input layer, hidden layer and output layer connected by weight matrices, and fit our training set to obtain the weight matrix between the input layer and hidden layer. That weight matrix serves as our features for our documents. Here is a picture showing the task:

image link

To train the neural network using our word pairs, we use one-hot encoding of all unique words among our word pairs for both input and output. The above picture shows the result of the testing phase, which is what would happen when we input a specific word like “ant.” The output is a softmax classifier that gives probabilities for choosing a random nearby word to “ant.” Again, the training phase uses one-hot encoding for both input and output.

The Hidden Layer

Based on the above picture, we set the hidden layer to be of size 300 neurons. This translates to setting our word vectors to be of size 300. In addition, the example states that there are 10000 unique words out of all of our word pairs, which serve as the size of our input space. As a result, the weight matrix will have 10000 rows and 300 columns. Similarly, our Word2Vec model will have input dimensions of 10000 X 300.

Obtaining Weights and Features

Given how large the weight matrix is, traditional gradient descent is going to be very slow. There are several workarounds to this:

  • Treat common phrases as single “words” in the model

  • Subsample frequent words to decrease size of training set

  • Limit impact of each training sample on model’s weights via “Negative Sampling”

Word Phrases

Turn common phrases such as “New York City” or “Boston Globe” into a single “word” like “NewYorkCity” or “BostonGlobe” to cut down training set, and thus reduce weight matrix size.

Subsampling Frequent Words

Looking back at our initial generation of word pairs, we saw that common words like “the” give us some problems. A word pair like (“dog”, “the”) doesn’t provide much context about “dog”, and our training set would be littered with more pairs such as (“the”, …) than we need to describe “the”. We can come up with a formula to help up determine the probability of keeping a word in our training set based on its frequency in our corpus. The higher its frequency, the less likely we will keep it.

Negative Sampling

Normally, a training sample will tweak all of the weights in a neural network. Given how large a weight matrix can be, we want to have a training sample only affect a small percentage of the weights. To do this, we go back to our training of word pairs using one-hot encoding. Now we identify a handful of “negative” words, which are words we want the network to output 0 for a given input. Then we will only update the respective weights for those “negative” words and the one “positive” word. As an example, we look at the word pair (“fox”, “quick”). “Quick” is our positive word and potential negative words would be “volleyball”, “ketchup”, and “gasoline.” Only weights according to these 4 words would be tweaked by the sample, which is hopefully a very small subset of our vocabulary
